Top 10 Coaching Questions

Effective coaching starts with clarity. Leaders often move rapidly between meetings with little mental reset time. Before you begin, pause and ask:

  • What do I know about this person?
  • What might they need from me right now?
  • Am I coaching, directing, supporting, or clarifying?

This prevents the “I’m pretending to coach but actually directing” dynamic that people easily sense, and that dissolves trust.

Very honest. Transparency builds trust and psychological safety. Try:

“I’m practicing my coaching skills and would like to use them in our conversation. It might feel a little awkward. If something feels off, please let me know.”

“I’d like to approach this conversation using coaching. It helps us explore options together. Are you open to that?”

That’s normal. Just as someone learning to ride a bike is wobbly at first, leaders learning coaching will be too. Acknowledge it openly—individuals appreciate honesty far more than a forced or covert approach.

  1. Acknowledge and respect their preference

    2. “Thank you for letting me know. I want this conversation to work for you, so let’s approach it in a way that feels comfortable.”

  2. Clarify what they do need to keep things collaborative

    3. “Absolutely. What kind of support would be most helpful today: information, direction, brainstorming together, or something else?”

  3. Offer a blend rather than “pure coaching” to remove pressure and keep the door open

    4. “We don’t need to use a full coaching approach. I can provide guidance, and we can explore options together as needed.”

  4. Normalize their reaction without judgment

    5. “It’s completely OK if coaching doesn’t feel like the right fit today. We can use another approach.”

  5. Reassure them about your role and intention

    6. “I’m still here to support you. Coaching is just one tool—if you’d prefer more direction or clarity, I can absolutely provide that.”

  6. Redirect with transparency to focus on purpose, not method

    7. “Thanks for saying that. I want us to have the most helpful conversation possible, so let’s shift gears. Here’s what I’m hoping we can accomplish today…”

Use straightforward wrap up questions such as:

  • “What’s the key thing you’re taking away today?”
  • “What action will you take next?”
  • “What support do you need from me?”
  • “What should we revisit next time?”

These work even when you can’t finish the full coaching model due to time.

Emotions belong in coaching. Avoid shutting them down. Instead:

  • Allow space (silence is OK).
  • Acknowledge: “I can see this is bringing something up.”
  • Ask: “What is this emotion telling you about the situation?”
  • Support forward movement.

Avoid counseling style questions like “Where does that emotion come from?” which take the conversation into territory outside a leader’s role.

Leaders often worry coaching requires long meetings. In reality, even 10–15 minutes can create clarity, reduce rework, and prevent future issues. The key is intentionality, not length. A short, focused conversation is far more effective than a long, unfocused one.

Go back to the basics: listening, questions, feedback, acknowledgment.

  • “What feels most important to focus on right now?”
  • “What’s one step you can take next?”
  • “What’s becoming clearer for you?”
