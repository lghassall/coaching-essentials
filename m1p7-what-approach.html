How do you know what approach to take?

There are many approaches to take to conversations. The trick is to pause and reflect on which approach is going to be most effective. To help you decide, ask yourself: Does the person need direction or information, or do they need to be empowered or motivated to take action?

You can also check in with the person: Do you want advice, need to vent, or want to talk through it together?

Most of the time, you’ll be hanging out in the middle of the continuum, using a mixture of both depending on what’s happening.

What about counselling?

Counselling and coaching share many of the same skills, but counselling is a specific set of skills led by a licensed, trained professional that supports people with healing, mental health, and emotional difficulties. Counselling often focuses on the past to understand current challenges. Coaching focuses on goals, development, and action and is oriented towards the present and future. You’re role in taking a coach approach is not to offer counselling. You would refer to appropriate supports where appropriate.

Alert! If a situation is beyond your role or expertise, seek support or refer it appropriately. For complex conflict, potential breaches of the standards of conduct or when addressing discrimination, bullying and harassment, contact your supervisor and the BC Public Service Agency. Resources such as the Conflict Management Office, Leadership Coaching, and HR Specialist can be accessed through AskMyHR (IDIR restricted). Non–core government employees should contact their agency.

Activity

You’re approached by your direct report/colleague to consult about a client. You know they have successfully completed the training. They’ve come to you several times asking similar questions and you begin to wonder what is going on.

Which approach would you take and why?

Select each approach for the outcome

Direct Approach

You inform them of the action to take and emphasize the importance of referring to the manual. The person walks away smiling, knowing what to do. They return with a similar question the following week.

Coaching Approach

You ask them what they would do in this situation and ask questions to explore the pros and cons of the decision. You support them to take that action because it is in line with policy/procedure.

The person walks away smiling, knowing what to do. They return with a similar question and a set of ideas they want to run by you to see if they are on track. Their frequency of asking similar questions reduces.

In this situation, the person has the information to make decisions and just needs the confidence to act independently. Although they returned with a similar question, they’re now bringing their own ideas and solutions instead of relying on you to provide them.
