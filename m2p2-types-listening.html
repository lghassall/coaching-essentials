Listening

There are two primary ways to listen: listening to respond and listening to understand.

Listening to Respond

When we’re listening to respond, we’re not really listening, we’re mostly just thinking about what we want to say next. You’ve probably experienced this before. It can feel like you’re not being heard or understood. Like water hitting a brick, nothing gets absorbed.

For many of us this is the default. Unless we’re intentional about building our listening skills, we tend to stay here. The challenge with listening to respond is that our attention is on ourselves, making it easy to be distracted and miss what the other person is really saying.

Signs you’re listening to respond:

  • Finishing the other person’s sentences
  • Thinking: “what should I say next” or “how can I prove my point” while they’re still talking

Listening to Understand (Active Listening)

When we listen to understand, we focus fully on the other person and are genuinely interested in what they have to say. We listen not just for information, but for meaning.

Signs you’re listening to understand:

  • Not interrupting
  • Paying attention to body language
  • Listening for both facts and underlying emotions
  • Asking thoughtful, relevant questions
  • Using your intuition
  • Listening for underlying emotions and information beyond the words
