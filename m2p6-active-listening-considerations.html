Active Listening Considerations

Active listening means tuning in to what the other person needs. By working together, we can create a safe, supportive, trusting environment where the other person can share freely. There are some key ingredients needed to create this environment:

1. Sensitivity to the other person’s identity, environment, experiences, values, and beliefs

When you’re sensitive to others, you’ll know that each person on the team requires a different approach.

Actionable Insight

Ask: Is there anything I should keep in mind about how I communicate with you to help keep our conversations respectful and supportive?

Ask: How do you like to receive feedback? How do you like to give feedback?

2. Develop self-awareness and intuition

This means understanding and recognizing your own context, including your own identity, environment, experiences, values, and beliefs, as this is the lens through which you look at the world and other individuals. Understanding these will help you keep your judgements and biases in check.

3. Cultivate Trust and Safety

Acknowledging and respecting the beliefs and perspective of individuals is important for cultivating trust and safety.

Actionable Insight

Ask: Are there any personal practices or preferences you want us to be mindful and respectful of?

Connection | IRBC

Cultural agility is an Indigenous relations behavioural competency that applies to all BC Public Service employees, no matter where you work. Cultural Agility is about noticing and readily adapting to cultural uniqueness to create a sense of safety for all. Review this IRBC and reflect on how it connects to your coaching.

Neurodiversity is the idea that there’s natural variation in the human brain that leads to differences in the way we think and behave. Types of neurodivergence include Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD), Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD), Dyslexia, and Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD).

Neurodivergent people may experience communication, processing, and reflection in unique ways. By slowing down, allowing extra processing time, and asking clarifying questions with curiosity (not assumption), you can create a more inclusive and supportive space where everyone feels understood and valued.

Learn more by enrolling in the Neurodiversity and Experiences of Neurodivergent People curated pathway.
